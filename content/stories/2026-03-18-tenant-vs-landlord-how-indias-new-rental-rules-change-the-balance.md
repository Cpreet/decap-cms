Renting a home in India has often been a tug of war. Tenants have complained about high deposits and sudden rent hikes, while landlords have struggled with delayed payments and long eviction battles.

However, much of this relationship has worked on trust, informal agreements, and uneven rules. Now, that long-standing equation is seeing a shift.

[A new rental framework based on the Model Tenancy Act](https://www.indiatoday.in/newsmo/video/the-truth-about-new-rent-laws-in-india-2026-2882447-2026-03-15?utm_source=global-search&utm_medium=global-search&utm_campaign=global-search), 2021 is trying to formalise how homes are rented in India. It aims to bring clarity, reduce disputes, and create a more balanced system for both tenants and landlords.

### WHAT HAS CHANGED IN RENTING

Rishabh Gandhi, Founder, Rishabh Gandhi and Advocates, said the biggest change is the move towards a formal system.

“The new rental framework attempts to formalise India’s largely informal rental housing market,” he said.

“One of the most important changes is that written tenancy agreements and their registration with a Rent Authority become central to the system.”

Earlier, many rental arrangements worked without proper documentation.

“Many agreements were oral or based on standard 11-month contracts that were rarely registered. The new framework pushes for formal contracts, clearly recorded terms, and greater transparency between landlords and tenants,” he added.

Adhiraj Harish, Partner at D.M. Harish & Co, said the intent of the law is to improve transparency and reduce disputes.

“The law aims to make landlord-tenant relationships more transparent and also create a system for quicker dispute resolution,” he said.

Several financial and operational aspects of renting are also being standardised.

“The law places limits on security deposits, generally restricting them to two months’ rent for residential properties and six months for commercial premises,” Gandhi said.

Harish added more detail to this.

“Security deposits are capped at two months’ rent for residential properties and six months for commercial ones,” he said.

“There is also a clear division of responsibilities for repairs between landlords and tenants.”

He added that the law also covers exceptional situations.

“It defines responsibilities in cases like force majeure events and makes it mandatory to register rental agreements with the Rent Authority within two months.”

“In practical terms, the shift is from a loosely regulated and largely landlord-driven system to a more structured contractual framework with clearer legal safeguards for both sides,” Gandhi said.

### TENANT RIGHTS VS LANDLORD PROTECTION

A key aim of the new rules is to balance the interests of both sides.

“From the tenant’s perspective, the law provides greater predictability and protection against arbitrary practices,” Gandhi said.

“Security deposits are capped, tenancy agreements must clearly specify rent, tenure, and responsibilities, and landlords cannot revise rent during the tenancy unless the agreement permits it and proper notice is given.”

Tenants also get stronger legal backing.

“Tenants gain procedural safeguards because eviction must now follow legally recognised grounds and disputes can be brought before specialised rent authorities instead of ordinary civil courts,” he added.

At the same time, landlords also gain protections that were often missing earlier.

“Landlords now have clearer rights to recover possession of their property, to take action against tenants who default in rent payment, and to enforce contractual terms,” Gandhi said.

“If a tenant overstays after the tenancy expires, the law allows landlords to claim enhanced rent or compensation.”

Harish underlined this provision.

“If a tenant fails to vacate after the agreement ends, the landlord can charge enhanced rent, which can go up to four times the monthly rent,” he said.

“The overall intention is to balance interests by protecting tenants from unfair practices while ensuring landlords are not trapped in endless tenancy disputes,” Gandhi added.

### EVICTION RULES ARE NOW CLEARER

Eviction has long been one of the biggest flashpoints in rental disputes.

Under the new framework, the conditions are clearly defined.

“Eviction can take place only on clearly defined legal grounds,” Gandhi said.

“These include non-payment of rent or accumulation of arrears, misuse of the premises, subletting without consent, causing structural damage, or making illegal alterations.”

Eviction is also allowed in specific situations.

“It may also be permitted if the property is required for major repairs, reconstruction, or other legitimate purposes that cannot be carried out while the tenant remains in possession,” he added.

This marks a shift from earlier systems.

“Older rent control laws in several states heavily favoured tenants and often allowed them to remain indefinitely as long as they continued paying controlled rent,” Gandhi said.

“The newer framework attempts to restore a more balanced system by recognising the landlord’s ownership rights while still ensuring that eviction happens only through due legal process.”

### FASTER DISPUTE RESOLUTION

Another major change is how disputes are handled.

“The new framework creates a specialised institutional structure to deal with rental disputes,” Gandhi said.

“At the first level is the Rent Authority, which registers tenancy agreements and handles routine disputes.”

“Appeals or more serious disputes can be taken to a Rent Court, and a further appeal lies before a Rent Tribunal.”

Harish also pointed to the role of the tribunal.

“The law introduces a Rent Tribunal for redressal of disputes,” he said.

This system is expected to reduce delays.

“This is designed specifically for tenancy matters rather than sending such disputes to ordinary civil courts, where cases often take years,” Gandhi said.

However, both experts flagged a key concern.

“The actual effectiveness will depend on how actively state governments implement these institutions and whether adequate administrative capacity is created,” Gandhi added.

### RENT INCREASE RULES

The new framework also brings clarity on rent hikes.

“Landlords can still increase rent, but the increase must follow a structured legal process,” Gandhi said.

“Rent revisions must usually be specified in the tenancy agreement itself, often through an escalation clause.”

This is meant to avoid sudden hikes.

“The landlord must give advance written notice before implementing a rent increase,” he said.

“This allows the tenant to accept, renegotiate, or vacate the premises.”

“If the tenant disputes the increase, the matter can be taken before the Rent Authority.”

### STATE RULES WILL STILL MATTER

One important point is that the law is not automatically the same across India.

“Land is a state subject under the Constitution, and therefore states may implement these rules in a varied form,” Harish said.

“States such as Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh and Assam have already proceeded to implement this.”

This means the impact will differ across regions.

“Each state law has its own nuances,” he said.

He explained this using Maharashtra as an example.

“In Maharashtra, the Maharashtra Rent Control Act currently governs landlord-tenant relationships, including systems like the pagdi model and leave and licence arrangements.”

“In cities like Mumbai, most rentals are already covered under registered leave and licence agreements, with standard templates and online registration.”

He added that the transition may not be straightforward.

“It would have to be seen how the legislature and executive achieve a balance between existing laws and the new tenancy framework.”

“Similarly, each state would have to carefully implement the law taking into account its existing laws and local conditions.”

### WHAT IT MEANS FOR TENANTS AND LANDLORDS

For tenants, the changes mean lower deposits, clearer agreements, and better protection against sudden actions.

For landlords, the new rules bring clearer rights over their property, better legal backing in disputes, and safeguards against tenants who overstay or default.

But the bigger shift is structural.

India’s rental market is slowly moving away from informal, trust-based arrangements towards a system built on written contracts, defined responsibilities, and legal clarity.

Whether this finally reduces friction between tenants and landlords will depend not just on the law, but on how well it is implemented on the ground.
